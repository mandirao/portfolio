## 02 · Career Chapters

### Chapter 1: The Creative Technologist (1997–2011)

Born into tech curiosity — dad was soldering motherboards and bringing home Photoshop 4 before most households had the internet. Taught myself HTML, CSS, and JavaScript from Webmonkey tutorials at 14. Built websites for friends. Internship at OMSI in front-end web using Flash and Dreamweaver. Graduated high school in 2001 into the dot-com crash.

Rebuilt through PSU's Honors College, painting major turned graphic designer. DC internship: gallery website for the Corcoran, printed materials for an arts logistics org. Meanwhile, seven years in food service — host to bartender to assistant manager at Applebee's, City Grill, Aztec Willies — teaching customer experience, composure under pressure, and reading a room.

Moved to NYC in 2010 with a futon, a design portfolio, and a lot of hustle. Freelanced design for actors and theater companies. Got recruited into the Apple Store by a connection. Began phasing out restaurant work.

### Chapter 2: The Designer Finding Her Edge (2012–2018)

**Tough Mudder (2012–2014):** First real design job. Differentiated immediately — graphic design degree + 15 years of coding + fast production across the full Adobe suite. Created the Murderella brand from scratch. Was offered manager role when her boss left, then was poached by Blue Apron.

**Blue Apron (2014–2017):** First full-time graphic designer on the marketing side. Led without authority. Wrangled creative process, naming conventions, digital asset management. Learned art direction, editorial design, infographics, animated email. Got mentored by a real creative director for the first time — design skills accelerated dramatically. Then laid off.

**The pivot moment:** A former colleague connected her to a client in Denver. After months of freelance work, the CEO said: "We think you're not just a designer. You're a product manager." The natural tendency to roadmap designs by feasibility — "we'll build this layer, validate, then build xyz" — had been product thinking all along. Moved to Denver. Shifted career.

### Chapter 3: The Product Leader (2018–present)

**Verblio (2018–Jan 2023):** 4.5 years as Head of Product & Design (Director → VP) at a bootstrapped two-sided content marketplace. Built the product and design organization from zero. Hired and developed a team of four. Drove 5x revenue growth without funding. Led strategy, culture, OKRs, and cross-functional execution at the executive level while staying hands-on IC.

**Sunday (April 2023–present):** Senior PM on a lawn care subscription platform. Inherited a broken funnel, built it into a machine. In year 1: Led 60+ A/B tests. Drove 115% CVR improvement. Delivered $1.2M over revenue goal. Earned "best steerco in company history" recognition from the President. Currently designing the v1 AI feature (Sunny AI photo upload) for the funnel. Psychologically challenging environment — but extraordinary proof of resilience and impact under constraint.

### The Need Algorithm — Product Sense + UX Instinct

**Core claim:** A 3-day UX fix drove a 30-point improvement that years of backend work couldn't.

Writers weren't changing behavior after a major algorithm update because nothing in the UI told them it had changed. Watched FullStory for 10 minutes. Saw the problem immediately. Changed numeric 'need' scores (1–5) to plain language (Lite → On Fire). Added a competition indicator when customers already had submissions. Rewrote helper text. Released a Pendo guide. Three days. Content delivered under 96h jumped from 60% to 90% — a trend that held for 2+ years.

**What it proves:** I don't wait for a sprint to solve a problem I can see clearly. UX is a growth lever, not a polish layer.

---

### The Writer Dashboard — Full PM Lifecycle + Risk Mitigation

**Core claim:** Rebuilt a brittle, broken experience into a trusted product — then learned the real risk wasn't the product.

Old Rails homepage: inaccurate data, broken features, un-iterable code. Led full discovery → design → story writing → QA → phased beta launch. Usability tested with writers. Released with rollback option to de-risk change resistance. Metrics improved. Writers hated it. Forum exploded. The product worked — the launch didn't. Learned: change management is product work too. Within 6 months, old version sunsetted with near-zero holdouts.

**What it proves:** I can run the full product lifecycle, and I treat failure as the beginning of the real insight.

### Sunny AI — AI Product Design (Current)

**Core claim:** Actively designing v1 AI feature for a consumer acquisition funnel.

The current lawn funnel asks leads to self-report yard conditions — a step with high drop-off and low data quality. Designed a photo-upload AI analysis feature: lead uploads a yard photo, vision model returns structured assessment (weeds, bare spots, pet damage), lead confirms or adjusts before data writes to the funnel. Spec includes image compression, graceful fallback to manual flow, confidence thresholds, correction rate tracking, and a shared analysis service pattern for all future use cases.

**What it proves:** I'm not learning about AI product — I'm doing it right now, with the right questions and the right systems thinking.

### The OKR Overhaul — Stakeholder Alignment + Change Management

**Core claim:** Used ADKAR to build top-down focus at a company that historically added goals but never subtracted them.

Bootstrapped company with the appetite of a well-funded startup. Too many OKRs, short memory, DMs lobbing urgent priorities over the fence. Used change management (ADKAR) to redesign the goal-setting process: affinity-mapped brainstorm, explicit 'what we will NOT do' list, frontloaded ride-or-die objectives. Designed 'The Map' (strategy status) and 'The Landscape' (early signal) as two new product council meeting formats. Result: fewer DMs, more trust, more efficient tradeoff conversations, and the only OKRs that slipped were the ones nobody mourned.

**What it proves:** I can change organizational behavior, not just ship product.

## 07 · What I Believe

**On AI and the PM craft:**
AI executes your thinking. It doesn't replace it. The bottleneck isn't the model — it's the human architecture behind the prompt. The PMs who will matter in this era aren't the ones who can push code to GitHub. They're the ones who can think clearly about what to build, for whom, and why — and work fluidly enough with the tools to not be the bottleneck.

**On design and product:**
UX is a growth lever, not a polish layer. A product can be technically correct and completely unusable. The goal is always to reduce friction to the thing that matters — for the user and for the business. Those two things are almost always the same thing, if you're asking the right questions.

**On teams:**
The best hire isn't always the most credentialed. Growth mindset, curiosity, and accountability at $60k will outperform entitled seniority at $150k. I'd rather build a team that proves itself than inherit a team that rests on its reputation.

**On failure:**
Disappointment is the beginning of success. I've launched things that worked technically and failed culturally (Writer Dashboard). I've led projects that took longer than they should (Multipass). I've taken jobs I knew were wrong fits (Sunday). Every one of these is an insight I carry forward, not a wound I nurse.
